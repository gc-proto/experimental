Hantaviruses

Information for Canadians about hantaviruses, including current situation, symptoms, prevention, travel risks and guidance for healthcare professionals.

Current situation

Public health authorities are monitoring international cases of Andes virus in South America. The risk to the general public in Canada remains low.

Most hantavirus infections are linked to exposure to infected rodents or their droppings. Rare person-to-person spread has been documented with Andes virus.

If you recently travelled to affected areas in Argentina or Chile and develop symptoms, seek medical attention and tell your healthcare provider about your travel history.

Current situation

Risk in Canada

The overall risk of hantavirus infection in Canada is low. Most Canadian cases are associated with exposure to infected deer mice in rural or wilderness settings.

International outbreaks

Public health authorities are monitoring Andes virus activity in parts of South America, including Argentina and Chile.

Monitoring and surveillance

The Public Health Agency of Canada works with provinces, territories and international partners to monitor hantavirus activity and investigate potential cases.

Symptoms and treatment

Symptoms

Symptoms may appear 1 to 8 weeks after exposure.

  • fever
  • fatigue
  • muscle aches
  • headache
  • nausea
  • shortness of breath
  • cough

Seek medical attention if you:

  • develop symptoms after rodent exposure
  • recently travelled to affected areas in South America
  • had close contact with someone diagnosed with Andes virus

Tell your healthcare provider about:

  • recent travel history
  • exposure to rodents or rodent droppings
  • cleaning enclosed spaces such as cabins or sheds
  • contact with a confirmed case

Treatment

There is no specific treatment or vaccine for hantavirus infection. Early medical care can improve outcomes. Severe illness may require hospitalization and intensive care.

Spread, prevention and risks

How hantaviruses spread

Transmission type Most hantaviruses Andes virus
Spread through rodents Yes Yes
Person-to-person spread Not known Rare but documented
Main geographic area Americas Argentina and Chile

Prevention

At home

  • seal holes and gaps where rodents can enter
  • store food securely
  • avoid sweeping or vacuuming rodent droppings
  • ventilate enclosed spaces before cleaning

Camping and outdoor activities

  • avoid sleeping near rodent nests
  • store food in sealed containers
  • use clean, well-maintained accommodations

Travel

  • follow local public health advice
  • avoid rodent-infested areas
  • monitor for symptoms after travel

People at higher risk

  • campers and hikers
  • agricultural and forestry workers
  • people cleaning cabins, sheds or barns
  • travellers to outbreak regions

Travel and borders

Before travel

  • check travel health notices
  • avoid accommodations with signs of rodent activity
  • learn how to safely clean enclosed spaces

After travel

Monitor for symptoms for several weeks after returning from affected areas.

Seek medical care if symptoms develop and tell your healthcare provider about your travel history.

Andes virus

Andes virus is a type of hantavirus found mainly in Argentina and Chile.

Unlike most hantaviruses, rare person-to-person transmission has been documented.

How Andes virus spreads

  • exposure to infected rodents or rodent droppings
  • close contact with an infected person in rare cases

Risk to Canadians

The risk to the general public in Canada remains low. Most risk is associated with travel to affected areas and exposure to rodents.

Frequently asked questions

Can hantavirus spread between people?

Most hantaviruses do not spread between people. Rare person-to-person spread has been documented with Andes virus.

Is there hantavirus in Canada?

Yes. Rare cases occur in Canada, usually linked to exposure to infected deer mice.

Is it safe to travel to South America?

Follow local public health guidance and take precautions to avoid rodent exposure.

Is there a vaccine?

There is currently no approved vaccine for hantavirus infection in Canada.
